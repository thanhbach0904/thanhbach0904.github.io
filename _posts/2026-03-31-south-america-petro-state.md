When Iranian crude disappeared from global markets following the escalation of sanctions and conflict, oil traders needed alternatives quickly. The beneficiary no one expected is a country more often associated with political dysfunction than energy exports.

## The windfall

Oil revenue flowing into the national treasury has doubled in two years. Infrastructure projects mothballed during leaner times are being revived. The government, once borrowing at punishing spreads to finance basic operations, has paid down external debt and rebuilt foreign reserves.

The numbers are remarkable. Production has risen by nearly 40% over three years, driven by foreign investment — much of it from Asian national oil companies eager for stable supply outside the Middle Eastern corridor — and by improved management of ageing fields.

## The infrastructure gap

The prosperity is real but fragile. The country's refining capacity remains limited, meaning it exports crude and reimports refined products at a markup. Roads, ports, and pipelines designed for lower throughput are straining. Skilled labour is scarce, and salary inflation in the oil sector is spilling into the broader economy.

## The resource curse, revisited

Development economists will recognise the pattern. Oil windfalls, absent strong institutions, tend to entrench existing power structures rather than build new ones. Fiscal discipline is hard to maintain when revenues are high; when commodity cycles turn, the damage is severe.

Whether this country can convert its geological luck into durable development depends less on oil prices than on institutional choices — transparent governance, investment in education, and diversification away from hydrocarbons — that are difficult in any political environment, and harder still in one where sudden wealth concentrates power.

The boom is real. The question is who it ultimately serves.
