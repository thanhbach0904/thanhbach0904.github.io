The Indian rupee has depreciated sharply over the past year, breaching levels that the Reserve Bank of India had spent considerable reserves defending. For the Modi government, which has staked much of its political identity on economic competence and the promise of a developed India by 2047, the currency's decline is an unwelcome complication.

## The macro mechanics

The rupee's weakness reflects several converging pressures. A strong dollar — itself a product of US interest rate resilience — makes most emerging market currencies look weak in comparison. India's trade deficit, persistently wide due to oil imports, adds fundamental selling pressure on the currency. And portfolio flows, which flooded into Indian equities during the bull market of the early 2020s, have partially reversed as global risk appetite recedes.

The RBI has intervened repeatedly, deploying foreign exchange reserves to smooth volatility if not arrest the trend. Reserves, while substantial, are not infinite.

## Who loses

Currency depreciation is not uniformly harmful. India's technology services exporters — earning dollars and paying rupee-denominated costs — benefit. But the Indian economy is a net importer of energy, and a weaker rupee raises the domestic cost of oil directly, feeding into transport, manufacturing, and consumer prices across the board.

For ordinary voters, this translates into higher petrol prices and costlier cooking gas — both politically sensitive items that the government has periodically subsidised at great fiscal cost.

## The political dimension

India faces state elections in three major states this year. The government's ability to manage the inflation narrative — already complicated by food price spikes — becomes harder with each fall in the currency.

The rupee's decline is a macroeconomic problem. But in an election year, economics is always also politics.
