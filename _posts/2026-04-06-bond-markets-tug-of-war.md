Bond markets are caught in a vice. On one side, persistent inflation keeps pushing yields upward as investors demand compensation for eroded purchasing power. On the other, mounting recession fears drag yields back down as capital flees to safety.

This tension — rare in its symmetry — has created some of the most volatile trading conditions in government debt since the 2008 financial crisis.

## The inflation argument

Central banks have been fighting inflation since 2022. Despite aggressive rate hikes, services inflation — driven largely by wages — has proved stickier than models predicted. The Federal Reserve's preferred inflation gauge, core PCE, remains above its 2% target.

> "The last mile of disinflation is always the hardest," remarked one Fed governor, citing historical parallels to the 1970s.

As long as inflation stays elevated, bond investors have little reason to accept lower yields. Locking in a 10-year Treasury at 4.2% only makes sense if you believe inflation will average below that over a decade.

## The recession argument

Yet growth indicators tell a darker story. The yield curve — the spread between 2-year and 10-year Treasuries — inverted deeply in late 2024 and has only recently normalized. Historically, inversions precede recessions by 12–18 months with near-perfect reliability.

Consumers are squeezed. Credit card delinquencies have risen to 2009 levels. Housing starts have slumped. Manufacturing PMIs across the G7 are in contraction.

## What happens next

The resolution of this tug of war will depend heavily on labour markets. If unemployment rises sharply, the recession camp wins: yields will fall, central banks will pivot, and bonds will rally. If the job market holds, inflation fears re-assert themselves.

For now, markets are doing what they always do in the face of genuine uncertainty: moving violently in both directions and calling it *price discovery*.
