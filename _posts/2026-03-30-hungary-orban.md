For sixteen years, Viktor Orbán has been the dominant force in Hungarian politics, methodically reshaping the country's institutions to entrench his Fidesz party's grip on power. Courts, media, electoral rules — each has been adjusted in ways that make his removal progressively harder.

Yet a convergence of pressures is creating what analysts of Hungarian politics describe as the most credible challenge to Orbán's dominance since he returned to power in 2010.

## The economic cracks

Hungary's economy, once a Fidesz strong suit, is sputtering. Inflation, which peaked above 25% in 2023, has fallen but remains elevated relative to the EU average. Real wages fell for two consecutive years. Foreign direct investment, which Orbán cultivated through tax incentives and industrial policy, has slowed as geopolitical uncertainty and EU disputes over rule of law create hesitation among major multinationals.

The government's fiscal position has also tightened. Brussels has withheld significant EU cohesion funds over rule-of-law concerns, creating a gap the Hungarian state has struggled to fill.

## The opposition's moment

For the first time in years, the Hungarian opposition has a figure who commands genuine public attention: a pragmatic mayor from Budapest's suburbs whose approval ratings have outpaced all previous Fidesz challengers. He has deliberately avoided the culture-war terrain where Orbán excels, focusing relentlessly on economic management and corruption.

## What would have to go right

Winning remains genuinely difficult. The electoral system Fidesz designed systematically advantages the incumbent. Rural constituencies, where Orbán's support is deepest, are over-represented. State media amplifies the government's message to the exclusion of almost all else.

The window is narrow. Whether the opposition can climb through it is the most consequential political question in central Europe this year.
