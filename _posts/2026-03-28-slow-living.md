At some point productivity stopped being a means and became an end. The shift is visible everywhere: in the language we use ("I need to optimise my morning routine"), in the products we buy (sunrise alarm clocks, meal prep containers, focus-enhancing supplements), and in the guilt that attaches to an afternoon spent without discernible output.

The result is a peculiar kind of exhaustion — not the satisfying tiredness that follows hard work, but the hollow fatigue of a person who has been efficiently busy without knowing why.

## The colonisation of leisure

The productivity gospel has not stopped at the boundaries of work. It has annexed the rest of life. Exercise is now quantified, tracked, and optimised. Friendships are maintained on scheduling apps. Reading has become something you "do" via 12-minute summaries. Even sleep is subject to measurement and improvement via wearables that grade your performance each morning.

There is something deeply strange about receiving a score for your unconscious hours.

## What is lost in the optimisation

Certain human experiences are structurally incompatible with efficiency. Grief cannot be processed on a timeline. Friendship deepens through unhurried, purposeless time together — the long dinners that run past midnight, the walks without destinations. Great books resist skim-reading. Skill in a craft requires the slow accumulation of bad attempts.

When we optimise these things, we do not get more of them. We get hollow simulacra that occupy the same calendar slots while delivering none of the substance.

## The case for inefficiency

Living at the wrong speed — too slow for the prevailing culture — is an act of quiet resistance and, perhaps, sanity. It means doing some things badly, slowly, and without measurable outcome. It means treating time as a medium in which life occurs, rather than a resource to be allocated.

This is not easy advice to take. The pressure to account for your hours is social and institutional as much as psychological. But the people who seem most fully alive are rarely the most efficient ones.

They are the ones who still have time to be surprised.
