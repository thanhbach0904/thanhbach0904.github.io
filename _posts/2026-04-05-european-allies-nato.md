In Brussels, in Berlin, and in Warsaw, officials are having a conversation they never expected to have: what does European defence look like without the United States?

The NATO alliance was built on the premise of American primacy — the nuclear umbrella, the forward-deployed troops, the unmatched logistics. For seven decades, that premise held. Today it is under serious strain.

## The drift

The signals have been accumulating. Washington has grown increasingly impatient with European defence spending, with most NATO members still falling short of the 2% of GDP target despite years of cajoling. More troubling to European capitals is the tone: senior American officials have openly questioned whether Article 5 — the mutual defence clause — would be invoked for countries that "don't pay their bills."

This transactional framing of a security alliance built on shared values is deeply alarming to European policymakers. Alliance commitments that are conditional are not, in their view, credible deterrents.

## What Europe is building

In response, the European Union has accelerated defence cooperation. The European Defence Fund has increased disbursements. Joint procurement of ammunition and air-defence systems has moved from proposal to practice. Germany has enacted historic constitutional changes to fund a €100bn military modernisation programme.

Yet the gap remains enormous. Europe's collective defence industry is fragmented, under-capitalised, and — with some notable exceptions — technologically behind American manufacturers. Replacing the US military presence overnight is not possible; replacing it over a decade is ambitious.

## The credibility problem

The deeper issue is strategic credibility. Deterrence depends not just on capability but on will. An adversary calculating the cost of aggression against Estonia or Latvia asks not "can Europe fight back?" but "will it?"

That question has a more uncertain answer today than at any point since the Cold War's end.
