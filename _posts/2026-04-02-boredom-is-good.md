At some point in the past decade, boredom became almost technologically impossible. There is no queue, no waiting room, no idle minute that cannot now be filled by a glowing rectangle offering infinite content. We have optimised away the empty spaces between things.

Something important has been lost.

## The neuroscience of wandering minds

Research in cognitive science suggests that the brain's default mode network — the neural circuitry that activates when we are *not* focused on an external task — plays a crucial role in creativity, memory consolidation, and self-reflection. This network is suppressed by task-focused attention and, crucially, by passive media consumption.

In other words: watching videos on your phone while waiting for coffee does not rest your brain. It keeps it in a low-engagement but externally directed state that crowds out the very processes associated with insight and imagination.

> "The mind needs fallow periods as much as fields do," wrote the psychologist Mary Helen Immordino-Yang in her research on daydreaming and learning.

## What boredom produces

Many of the most consequential ideas — in science, in art, in business — have reportedly arrived not during focused work sessions but in the shower, on long walks, or while staring out of train windows. These are precisely the conditions that boredom, properly understood, creates.

Boredom is not the absence of thought. It is thought unconstrained by external demands. It is the mind doing work we cannot see or direct.

## The discipline of doing nothing

The practical implication is uncomfortable: if creative and reflective thinking matters to you, you must protect space in your life for it, actively and deliberately. This means resisting the pull of your phone. It means allowing gaps to remain gaps.

This is harder than it sounds. The urge to fill silence is not laziness but anxiety — a discomfort with the self that constant stimulation allows us to avoid.

Learning to be bored is, in this sense, a form of courage.
